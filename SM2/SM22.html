Strategy & Organization — Study Notes

Strategy, Alignment & Execution

A working set of notes on why strategy drifts from intention to reality, how the McKinsey 7S framework and the Balanced Scorecard diagnose and measure execution, and what the McKinsey, Tata Nano, and ICICI cases reveal about getting it right.

Part One

The Reality of Strategic Drift

Takeaway: what you plan is rarely what you execute.

Implementation is a continuous cycle, not a handoff

The Myth

Strategy is a one-time change-management process, dictated from the top down and handed off from formulation to implementation.

The Reality

Tactical adaptations cut across silos and require systemic, daily alignment. The faster an organization cycles through hypothesis, experimentation, learning, and action, the stronger its competitive advantage.

The Anatomy of Failure

"Why do so many strategies fail?"

A brilliant business model cannot survive broken interdependencies. The root cause of strategic failure is a lack of holistic vision: leaders fixate on a single element — a new technology, an unmet need, or a market position — while ignoring the vital interdependencies that make a strategy functional over time.

Part Two — Diagnostic Framework

The McKinsey 7S Framework

The core challenge: structure is not organization. The formal chart focuses exclusively on reporting lines and visible hierarchy; the actual organization is a complex system of human interactions, unwritten rules, and shared beliefs.

The seven elements

ElementMeaningKey questionExample — Amazon AI transformation
StrategyLong-term plan to achieve competitive advantageWhere are we going?Become an AI-first e-commerce company
StructureOrganization design and reporting relationshipsWho reports to whom?Create a dedicated AI division reporting to the CTO
SystemsDaily processes and proceduresHow is work done?AI-powered warehouse management and CRM systems
Shared ValuesCore beliefs and organizational cultureWhat do we believe in?"Customer obsession" and innovation
StyleLeadership behaviourHow do leaders manage people?Leaders encourage experimentation and data-driven decisions
StaffEmployees and talentWho works here?Recruit AI engineers and data scientists
SkillsOrganizational capabilitiesWhat are we good at?Machine learning, cloud computing, logistics optimization

Hard vs. soft elements

The Hard Triangle
Strategy — the plan for how the organization will compete and allocate resources.
Structure — how the organization is arranged: reporting lines, divisions, centralized vs. decentralized.
Systems — the formal and informal procedures that keep the organization running (IT, capital budgeting, performance reviews).

Easy to document and redesign. Directly controlled by management.

The Soft Square
Shared Values — the guiding concepts and core purpose that go beyond conventional financial objectives (the core).
Style — how leadership actually behaves and spends its time, not just what it says in town halls.
Staff — the people, and the mechanisms by which they are recruited, developed, and promoted.
Skills — the distinctive capabilities and dominating attributes the organization has built over time.

Embedded in culture. Difficult to mandate; requires sustained behavioural change.

The gap between a company's stated goals and actual execution is usually widest in the soft elements.

Example — Tesla's EV expansion

7S elementExample
StrategyBecome the global leader in electric vehicles
StructureEstablish dedicated Battery and Energy divisions
SystemsIntroduce automated Gigafactory production systems
Shared ValuesSustainability and innovation
StyleElon Musk promotes speed and innovation
StaffRecruit battery engineers and AI experts
SkillsExpertise in battery technology and autonomous driving

Key learning: changing only Strategy is not enough. If Tesla decides to become an AI company but employees aren't trained, culture resists innovation, and systems remain outdated — the strategy will fail.

Strategy maps

Strategy maps provide a visual representation of critical objectives and the relationships between them — demonstrating cause-and-effect links where improvements in one area (e.g. employee capability) lead to outcomes in another (e.g. revenue), detecting gaps in strategies implemented at lower levels, and making cause-and-effect relationships explicit and testable.

Classic reference example: Mobil's strategy map links its Learning & Growth initiatives (functional excellence, new technology, aligned personal goals) through Internal Process (non-gasoline products, dealer segmentation, cost leadership, safety) and Customer perspective (clean, safe, quality, speedy — "delight the consumer") up to the Financial objective of increasing return on capital employed.

How the two connect

StepMcKinsey 7S FrameworkBalanced Scorecard
1Align Strategy, Structure, Systems, Shared Values, Style, Staff, and Skills—
2Ensure the organization is capable of executing the strategy—
3—Translate the strategy into objectives across Financial, Customer, Process, and Learning perspectives
4—Measure performance using KPIs and targets
5—Monitor results and continuously improve execution

In short: 7S gets the organization capable of executing a strategy; the Balanced Scorecard tells you whether it's working.

Why Do So Many Strategies Fail? — David J. Collis

Strategy fails because managers optimize one part of the strategy landscape instead of integrating the whole. A good strategy must simultaneously create value, capture value, execute well, and continuously adapt.

Why strategies fail

ReasonExplanationExample
Focus only on opportunitiesExciting opportunities identified, execution ignoredMany startups with great ideas fail
Focus only on competitive advantageMature firms defend current position and miss disruptionKodak, Nokia
Poor implementationGood strategy, weak executionTesla production delays
No adaptationEnvironment changes faster than the firmBlockbuster
Lack of integrationBusiness model, positioning, and execution don't alignA common root cause of strategic failure

Mistakes of established companies

Too much focus on competition (Kodak), ignoring new business models for fear of cannibalization (Blockbuster), an annual planning mindset that leaves no room for experimentation (Nokia), and slow adaptation due to bureaucracy (BlackBerry).

Mistakes of entrepreneurs

Overestimating the value created and assuming demand guarantees profit (Tesla's early EV optimism), ignoring that high value attracts rivals (WhatsApp), scaling before building capability, and prioritizing product over execution.

Case Study

Tata Nano — Innovation Without Execution

Strategy, Shared Values, Staff, and Skills were strong; Structure and Systems weakened during implementation, causing execution to fail — a textbook illustration of Collis's argument that strategy fails when firms optimize one part of the landscape rather than the whole.

Governance & Firm Value — ICICI Bank

Governance is not a soft topic on the side of strategy — it feeds directly into the cost of capital and, through it, into firm value.

↓ Governance Risk
lower perceived risk
=
↓ Cost of Capital
lower required return
=
↑ Present Value
higher firm value

The governance value chain

Good Governance

Strong board oversight, ethical leadership, transparency, independent directors, proper disclosures. ICICI should have ensured Chanda Kochhar recused herself from any loan decision with a perceived conflict of interest.

↓
Lower Governance Risk

Investors believe fraud, corruption, and unethical decisions are less likely. The Videocon loan controversy instead signaled weak conflict-of-interest controls, and governance risk rose sharply.

↓
Lower Cost of Capital

Investors require a lower return because the company appears safer, and are willing to lend or invest at lower rates.

↓
Higher Present Value

Future cash flows are discounted at a lower rate, increasing the firm's value today — and, if governance is restored, market valuation and shareholder wealth improve.

Reading the four points

#Slide pointMeaningICICI example
01Valuation premiumCompanies with better governance receive higher market valuationsWorld-class governance would let ICICI command a higher price-to-book multiple
02Governance substitutes for weak lawsStrong internal governance compensates when external investor protection is limitedAn independent board and ethics committee can catch what regulation alone cannot
03Lower governance risk → lower cost of capitalInvestors perceive the firm as safer and demand lower returnsAfter reform, lenders and shareholders require a lower risk premium
04Lower cost of capital → higher present valueLower discount rates raise the present value of future cash flowsEven with unchanged profits, valuation improves as investors discount earnings less harshly
The valuation premium

The direct valuation-multiple premium for good governance is vastly higher in developing economies, where excellent firm-level governance acts as a critical substitute when local investor protection laws are weak.

The financial impact

Lowering governance risk directly limits downside exposure, effectively dropping the cost of capital — and that minimized capital cost mathematically drives a higher present value for the firm.
